Chinese Folk Melody Texture

by Yue Hao

Most folk songs of the Han Chinese ethnic group (汉族) are built on the same pentatonic scale (宫商角徵羽), yet tunes from the north and the south can sound worlds apart. Up on the wind-blown Loess Plateau (黄土高坡) of the Yellow River (黄河) valley, the xintianyou (信天游) ballads of Northern Shaanxi (陕北) ring out high and mournful, steeped in a vast, aching desolation; down in the water towns of Jiangnan (江南) — the land south of the lower reaches of the Yangtze River (长江) — the soft Wu dialect (吴侬软语) turns melody tender and lingering, delicate and refined. This project compares the two textures by quantifying the melody itself: it measures the semitone (chromatic) distance between every consecutive pair of notes in a theme, then shows it two ways — a melodic contour with each leap labeled and color-coded, and an interval-distribution histogram — so a region's musical "accent" becomes something you can see.

★ Headline finding

The Northern Shaanxi (陕北) signature — oscillating wide leaps: the tune springs a wide leap (a 4th, 5th, or more) and immediately answers it with another wide leap the opposite way — up, down, up, down — for a bold, far-carrying, heroic sound. On the contour you spot it instantly as a zigzag of orange/red boxes (orange = 4th/5th, red = wider) pointing alternately up then down. The key isn't just that the leaps are big — it's that a leap is answered by another leap, not smoothed over by small steps.

The Jiangnan (江南) signature is the mirror image: mostly 3rds and small steps, so the line stays smooth and close — gentle and delicate — with few or no orange boxes.

Shandong (山东) sits between the two — a third-texture extension (example: 《沂蒙山小调》): wide leaps do appear, but singly, set into largely stepwise motion, rather than chaining into the Shaanxi saw-tooth. Each interval is also named (e.g. Perfect 5th / P5), tying the picture back to the hypothesis.
